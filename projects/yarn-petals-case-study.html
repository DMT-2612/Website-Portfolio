Yarn Petals: Sales Reporting Workflow Redesign

Full portfolio case study.

1. Project Overview

Yarn Petals is an e-commerce crochet flower brand that needed a more reliable way to manage sales reporting during an ownership transition. Sales data existed across Shopify exports, manual Excel files, and social media order records, but the files did not follow one consistent structure.

The project delivered a redesigned reporting workflow: raw sales files were mapped into a universal dataset, and that dataset was used as the source for a one-page Power BI sales dashboard. The dashboard provided the owner with a clearer view of revenue, order count, customer count, sales channels, trends, product mix, animal designs, colours, occasions, and state-level demand.

The project was also an AI-assisted delivery case study. I owned the project direction, business rules, requirements, validation, and handover logic, while using AI tools to support technical execution such as Python logic, DAX guidance, dashboard iteration, and documentation.

2. Business Context

Yarn Petals had been operating for three years and tracking sales through multiple data sources. Shopify exports were messy, the 2025 manual order file did not match the previous structured dataset, and Instagram and Facebook orders lacked consistent order numbers.

The business risk was practical:

  • Sales performance could be reported incorrectly.
  • Manual cleaning created delay and human error.
  • Category analysis was difficult because product, animal, colour, and occasion values were inconsistent.
  • The new owner needed a process that could be repeated after handover.

The core challenge was not only to create a dashboard. The business needed a simple reporting workflow that connected raw sales records, transformation rules, a structured dataset, and an owner-friendly visual reporting layer.

3. My Role

My role was Business Analyst across the full project. I managed the problem framing, requirements, business rules, solution structure, dashboard planning, validation checks, and handover documentation.

Key responsibilities included:

  • Understanding the reporting problem from the owner's perspective.
  • Defining what the universal dataset needed to contain.
  • Documenting functional and non-functional requirements.
  • Clarifying scope boundaries, including what would not be automated.
  • Converting product and order logic into explicit business rules.
  • Using AI tools to accelerate technical build tasks while validating the business output.
  • Checking the final report against expected totals and privacy rules.

5. Requirements and Scope

The project objectives were to:

  • Create a universal structured dataset format.
  • Develop a Python-supported process to clean and map Shopify exports.
  • Standardise manual and social media orders.
  • Build an interactive Power BI dashboard.
  • Make the solution transferable and scalable for the new owner.

The solution was intentionally scoped for a small business handover. It did not include Shopify system restructuring, ERP integration, real-time API integration, Instagram or Facebook order capture automation, or accounting reconciliation.

Non-functional requirements focused on usability, maintainability, reliability, scalability, compatibility with Power BI, and protecting customer email data from public reporting views.

6. Workflow Redesign

The redesigned workflow followed four layers:

  1. Data input: Shopify CSV, manual Excel orders, and social media order records.
  2. Transformation: Python workflow in Google Colab to clean, map, standardise, and generate missing order numbers.
  3. Single source of truth: `Universal.xlsx`, sheet `Orders`, used as the reporting dataset.
  4. Reporting layer: Power BI dashboard using Orders, DimDate, Metrics, and Parameter tables.

This workflow reduced the dependency on manual spreadsheet fixes and gave the owner a clearer path for refreshing the dashboard when new sales files became available.

7. Business Rules

A major part of the project was converting messy category logic into explicit rules. These rules made the transformation process more consistent and easier to maintain after handover.

  • Blank product, animal, or colour values were standardised to `NG`.
  • Special customised bundles and selected high-price products were mapped into `Largest Bundle`.
  • Bud Bundle style variants were standardised into one `Bud Bundle` category.
  • Selected product names drove animal classification, such as Hana Katsu, Tonkatsu, Chickenkatsu, and Ebi Katsu.
  • Accessories such as graduation or birthday hats drove occasion classification.
  • Selected product families were mapped to couple-related occasion logic when no occasion was provided.

These rules were important because the dashboard would only be useful if categories were stable enough for filtering, distribution analysis, and trend review.

8. Dashboard Design

The final Power BI report was designed as a one-page executive sales dashboard. It used a Yarn Petals pink theme and focused on the information the owner would need most often.

Dashboard Area Purpose
KPI cards Show net sales, orders count, and total customers.
Slicers Filter by product, animal, colour, date, occasion, and state.
Parameter switch Allow charts to switch between net sales and orders count.
Trend chart Show monthly sales or order movement over time.
Channel and state visuals Compare where orders came from and where demand was located.
Distribution donuts Break down colour, product, animal, and occasion mix.

9. Validation and UAT

The dashboard was checked against known totals from the final dataset. The unfiltered validation baseline was:

  • Net Sales: $77,601.98 AUD.
  • Orders Count: 1,132 distinct orders.
  • Total Customers: 744 distinct customer names.
  • Date range: 2023-06-01 to 2026-01-27.

Additional checks covered slicer behaviour, chart switching through the parameter control, Year-Month trend sorting, no prior-year or YoY logic in the final scope, and customer email exclusion from all report visuals.

10. Business Value

The project created value by turning fragmented order records into a repeatable reporting system.

  • Reduced manual cleaning effort and reporting inconsistency.
  • Improved confidence in revenue, order, and customer reporting.
  • Created a structured handover asset for the new owner.
  • Made sales performance easier to analyse by channel, product, colour, animal, occasion, and state.
  • Provided documentation that explains the requirements, dashboard structure, measures, and validation approach.

12. Reflection

The key learning from this project was that AI can speed up technical delivery, but it still needs strong business direction. The most important work was not simply generating code or visuals. It was deciding what the business needed, what rules had to be followed, what should be excluded from scope, and how to verify that the final output was reliable.

For a small business reporting project, a practical solution is more valuable than a complex one. The final workflow focused on a simple data refresh path, clear category rules, a stable Power BI model, and a dashboard that supported owner-level decisions.
